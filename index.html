The Survival Paradox: Risk, Reward, and Run-Scoring

An objective analysis of how batting average, strike rate, and survival at the crease define greatness in professional cricket.

View Visualizations →

Problem Statement

In the world of cricket, fans constantly debate what makes a batsman truly "elite." Is it the ability to stay at the crease for long periods, or the speed at which runs are scored? Many assume that higher survival leads to better performance, but does the data show an increased risk for those who stay in longer? This project investigates the correlation between total runs, times out, and batting averages to cut through fan assumptions with hard numbers.

Research Questions

Question 1

Does a higher survival rate at the crease actually result in a higher career batting average?

Question 2

How do total runs accumulated correlate with the number of times a player is dismissed?

Question 3

Are there measurable differences in run accumulation between Indian and Overseas players?

Conclusion

The analysis reveals a "Survival Paradox": while scoring more runs is the goal, doing so inevitably increases the cumulative risk of dismissal. However, elite players are distinguished by their ability to maintain high averages even as their "Times Out" count rises. Furthermore, geographical data suggests that Indian players in this dataset show strong domestic consistency, proving that environment is a key factor in run production alongside individual skill.
